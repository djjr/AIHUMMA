# AIHUMMA: Why a New Program, and What Our Course Inventory Tells Us

*On the rationale for a new master's program, what we learned from mapping 65 existing courses, and how institutional resources might be recruited to support something genuinely new.*

---

## Two Reasons to Build This Program

AIHUMMA exists for two reasons that are equally important and mutually reinforcing.

The first is a workforce argument. The organizations doing the most consequential work in AI governance — CSET, GovAI, AI Now, CAIS, PAI, and a growing set of government offices, foundations, and think tanks — are understaffed with people who can operate fluently across technical, policy, and communications domains. The field is not short of smart people. It is short of people with a specific preparation: policy-analytical rigor, technical literacy sufficient for governance purposes, research methods matched to the actual craft of the field, and the writing skills to move things through institutional channels. Producing more such people is both a program opportunity and a genuine contribution.

The second reason is harder to reduce to an elevator pitch but ultimately more important: the decisions being made about AI systems right now will shape institutions, labor markets, political discourse, and concentrations of power for decades. Having more people in more organizations who are equipped to engage those decisions seriously — not as advocates for a predetermined position, but as analysts capable of forming and defending defensible views — matters. That's the "make the world better" case, stated plainly.

These two reasons point toward the same program design. A program that produces graduates who are genuinely competitive for the best positions in the field will also be producing people with the preparation to do the work well. The standards are not in tension.

## What Our Course Inventory Was For

We mapped 65 AI-related courses across our institution's schools and departments against a 47-code learning outcome taxonomy. It is worth being clear about why we did this, because it is easy to misread the exercise as a gap analysis aimed at patching deficiencies in the existing curriculum. That is not what it was.

The analysis served two purposes. The first was developmental: examining what existing courses actually teach — at what level, for which audiences, with what assumptions about prior knowledge — helped sharpen the taxonomy itself. It forced a distinction between outcomes that the field genuinely requires and outcomes that merely appear in syllabi because they're intellectually adjacent. The coverage data (how many of the 65 courses address each code, and how centrally) became a form of external validation for the taxonomy's priorities.

The second purpose was strategic: understanding our institution's landscape helps identify resources that might be recruitable. Faculty in departments whose AI-related courses are underenrolled may welcome a partner program that creates new demand for their expertise. Courses that address relevant content but for the wrong audience at the wrong level are potential building blocks for new required courses, developed collaboratively. Mapping what exists is not the same as being constrained by it — but knowing the terrain matters for navigating it.

## Why Existing Courses Cannot Assemble Into This Program

The coverage analysis shows that some outcomes AIHUMMA needs to build are genuinely scarce across our catalog. But the more fundamental reason a new program is necessary is not about what topics are or aren't covered somewhere. It is about package size, depth, and audience orientation.

A student with unlimited elective freedom and good advising could take existing courses that touch every topic AIHUMMA addresses. They would still not arrive at the same place. Here is why.

**The wrong package size.** A graduate course in AI ethics, designed for a mixed audience of engineers and policy students, might spend three weeks on the regulatory landscape, two on philosophical frameworks, two on bias and fairness, and one on governance institutions. That is the right set of topics in the wrong proportions for someone who needs to be able to conduct regulatory research. AIHUMMA's F2 (*Institutions, Governance, and the Regulation of Risky Technologies*) spends a full semester on the comparative governance question alone — using nuclear, pharmaceutical, aviation, and financial regulation as analytical lenses. No assembly of existing courses produces that depth in that configuration.

**The wrong audience orientation.** Technical AI courses at our institution are taught by and for engineers. The conceptual vocabulary, the assumed prior knowledge, the assignments, and the level of mathematical detail are calibrated for students who will build AI systems. This is not a deficiency — it is appropriate for their purpose. But a course taught for engineers is structurally different from a course teaching the same concepts to future policy analysts, even if the syllabus looks similar. AIHUMMA's F1 (*How AI Systems Work*) teaches technical AI literacy through the governance artifacts — model cards, capability evaluations, responsible scaling policies — that its graduates will actually encounter in their work. That is a different course, not a repackaged version of an existing one.

**The missing program arc.** The sequencing and scaffolding that make a coherent degree program are not properties of individual courses — they emerge from deliberate design across the curriculum. The skill of reading technical documents as arguments (F1) becomes the raw material for policy writing (F4) and reaches its highest application in the frontier AI risk seminar (A3). That arc does not exist in any available elective pathway. It has to be built.

## What the Analysis Does Tell Us About Program Design

Setting aside the gap-filling frame, our coverage analysis yields several findings that are directly useful for program development.

The first concerns where to concentrate required course development effort. The outcomes that appear rarely or not at all in the existing catalog — qualitative research methods for AI governance, the procedural-versus-structural ethics distinction, policy genre writing, historical technology governance analogies read as AI policy lenses — are the outcomes where AIHUMMA has no curricular competition and no institutional alternative. These are the outcomes the program must own, and they are also the outcomes most closely aligned with what the hiring organizations require. Required course development should concentrate here.

The second concerns where not to build. The outcomes our institution covers in ten or more existing courses — ML technical foundations, quantitative methods, broad AI ethics surveys, domain applications in health and business — are areas where AIHUMMA's elective guidance can point students toward genuinely strong existing options. Building required courses in these spaces would mean competing with established offerings for no clear educational gain, while consuming development resources better spent elsewhere.

The third concerns recruitment opportunities. Several outcomes that AIHUMMA needs at a secondary level are addressed well by courses in departments that may be receptive to collaboration: humanistic methods and AI and culture courses in the humanities and social science divisions, international affairs and policy programs, and design and human-computer interaction courses in engineering. Identifying these courses and their instructors early creates the possibility of coordinated advising, cross-listing arrangements, or collaborative course development that enriches the program without requiring AIHUMMA to build everything from scratch.

---

## The Case in Summary

AIHUMMA is not a patch for holes in our institution's AI curriculum. It is a new program designed from the ground up to produce a specific kind of professional — one equipped to work seriously on AI governance — at a moment when the need for such people is urgent and the existing educational infrastructure does not reliably produce them. Our course inventory informed that design and identified institutional resources worth recruiting. The finding that no existing pathway produces AIHUMMA graduates is a consequence of the program's ambition, not its primary justification.

The primary justification is simpler: this work matters, these people are needed, and we are well-positioned to build a program that produces them well.
