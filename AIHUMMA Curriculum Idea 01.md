## Foundation Layer: Four Core Courses
Everyone takes these regardless of track. They build the shared language of the cohort and ensure every graduate can function credibly in any of the target orgs.
### AIHUMMA-F1 · How AI Systems Work (For People Who Don't Build Them)
**3 credits | Semester 1**

The most important course in the program — and the hardest to design well. The goal is _functional literacy_, not technical competency. Students should finish able to:

- Read a capabilities evaluation report and understand what it's claiming and what it isn't
- Understand what "training," "fine-tuning," "RLHF," "alignment," "evaluation," and "deployment" mean well enough to use them correctly in policy writing
- Understand what makes LLMs different from earlier AI paradigms (rules-based, narrow AI) and why that matters for governance
- Recognize when technical claims in policy documents are overreaching or underselling

**Pedagogical design:** This course should never assign a textbook chapter explaining neural networks. Instead it assigns: model cards, capability evaluations (METR's own published evals), red-teaming reports, and the public documentation AI labs release about their systems. Students learn the concepts _through the governance artifacts_ rather than through technical instruction. The implicit lesson: these documents are arguments, not facts, and reading them critically is itself a skill.

**Key reading types:** METR's evaluation reports, Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy, OpenAI's preparedness framework, NIST's AI RMF, EU AI Act technical annexes. Not ML textbooks.

**Signature assignment:** A "capability assessment translation" — take a recent evaluation report and write a 500-word plain-language brief for a fictional Congressional staffer who has 10 minutes before a hearing.
### AIHUMMA-F2 · Institutions, Governance, and the Regulation of Risky Technologies

**3 credits | Semester 1**

This is where humanities training gets explicitly _leveraged_ rather than treated as background noise. The course treats AI governance as a problem in political economy and institutional design, using comparison cases from other high-risk technology governance domains.

**The central argument of the course:** AI governance isn't new — it's a new instance of recurring problems in governing powerful, dual-use, rapidly evolving technologies. We've done this before, imperfectly, with nuclear weapons, pharmaceuticals, financial instruments, aviation, and biosafety. What did we learn? What failed? What's actually new about AI?

**Case studies to anchor the course:**

- Nuclear: international inspection regimes, the IAEA, how verification works when you can't see inside the weapons
- Pharma: the FDA's emergence from disaster (thalidomide), pre-market approval, what "clinical trial" means as an institutional form
- Finance: post-2008 stress testing, the FSOC, systemic risk as a governance concept
- Aviation: the FAA's dual mandate (promote and regulate), what went wrong with 737 MAX, the limits of self-certification
- Biosafety: dual-use research of concern, the Asilomar conference, voluntary vs. mandatory moratoriums

**Why this matters for your curriculum targets:** GovAI, CAIS, and AI Now all produce work that draws on these analogies. A student who knows the IAEA inspections regime in detail is already fluent in a conversation that dominates technical AI governance. Your students arrive at GovAI already knowing the language.

**Signature assignment:** A "governance analogy memo" — identify which prior regulatory regime offers the best model for governing a specific AI risk (e.g., foundation model evaluation), argue for your choice, and explain where the analogy breaks down. This is exactly the genre GovAI Applied Track fellows produce.
### AIHUMMA-F3 · Ethics, Values, and AI: From Philosophy to Policy
**3 credits | Semester 1 or 2**

This is the course that most AI policy programs get wrong by making it too abstract. The goal here isn't to survey ethical frameworks — it's to understand how ethical arguments actually _function_ in policy processes. How do values get encoded in technical standards? Who gets to define "fairness" in an algorithmic system, and through what institutional processes? What's the difference between "AI ethics" as a corporate PR function and as a genuine governance discipline?

**Course arc:**

- **Part 1: The landscape.** What are the dominant ethical frameworks invoked in AI (consequentialism, rights-based, virtue ethics, care ethics) and what do they actually imply for governance? This is fast — 3 weeks, not a philosophy survey course.
- **Part 2: From values to standards.** How did "fairness" become a technical concept? What is algorithmic accountability and who enforces it? Case studies: COMPAS recidivism tool, facial recognition in law enforcement, hiring algorithms.
- **Part 3: Institutional ethics.** Constitutional AI, model cards, impact assessments, red-teaming — these are all ethical instruments. What theories of governance do they embody? What do they assume about where power sits and how it should be checked?
- **Part 4: The limits of ethics.** The "ethics washing" critique (Timnit Gebru, AI Now). When do ethics frameworks substitute for regulation rather than inform it? What does structural critique look like vs. procedural ethics?

**Signature assignment:** An "ethics instrument audit" — take a real AI ethics document (a company's AI principles, a government framework, a standards body's guidelines) and analyze it: What values does it encode? What does it leave out? Who wrote it and why? What would need to change for it to have teeth?
### AIHUMMA-F4 · Writing for Policy: Genre, Audience, and Argument
**3 credits | Semester 2**

This is the most unusual course in the program — pure genre training — and possibly the most important for placement. Policy writing is a distinct craft with conventions that humanities students don't automatically have, even if they write beautifully in academic modes.

**What policy writing is:** Short, structured, claim-forward, audience-calibrated, action-oriented. A policy memo is not an essay. A white paper is not a journal article. A congressional brief is not a blog post. An op-ed is not a think piece. Each has a form, a function, and a reader who is busy, skeptical, and consequential.

**Course structure:** Workshop-based. Every week students produce a short piece in a different genre and workshop it. The instructor reads real policy memos, testimony transcripts, government reports, and GovAI-style white papers alongside the student work.

**Genres covered:**
- The two-page policy memo (the workhorse of the field)
- The executive summary (often the only thing policymakers read)
- The white paper (long-form argument for a sophisticated but non-specialist audience)
- Congressional testimony and briefing documents
- The op-ed and public essay (translating technical or policy analysis for general readers)
- Grant proposals and program descriptions (for operations-track students)

**Signature assignment:** Students revise a piece from their F1 "capability assessment translation" assignment into three formats — a policy memo, an op-ed, and a one-paragraph executive summary — showing they understand how the same information gets shaped differently for different audiences and power relationships.
## Concentration Tracks
After the foundation, students choose one of two tracks based on where they want to land.
### Track A: Policy and Governance Research
_Targets: GovAI Research Track, AI Now, CSET Research Analyst, PAI policy roles_

**AIHUMMA-A1 · Comparative AI Regulation (3 credits)** Deep dive into the EU AI Act, the UK post-Brexit approach, the US Executive Order and its aftermath, China's algorithm and generative AI regulations, and emerging frameworks in India, Brazil, and the Global South. Not just doctrinal comparison — comparative institutional analysis. Why did the EU produce binding law while the US produced voluntary frameworks? What does that tell us about the political economy of AI governance in each jurisdiction?

**AIHUMMA-A2 · Research Methods for AI Governance (3 credits)** The craft of producing the kind of research GovAI and CSET actually publish. Qualitative methods: elite interviewing, document analysis, process tracing. Quantitative literacy: reading empirical papers, understanding what datasets can and can't show, basic descriptive statistics without requiring stats prerequisite. How to write a research proposal. How to go from a policy question to an answerable research question. This course treats Miles Brundage's career arc as a case study — how do you produce policy-relevant research that practitioners actually use?

**AIHUMMA-A3 · Seminar: Frontier AI Risk and Governance (3 credits)** The most intellectually demanding course in the program. Reads the serious literature on catastrophic and transformative AI risk: Ord, Bostrom, Christiano, Davidson, Yudkowsky (as a primary source, read critically), and the empirical evaluation literature from METR and Apollo Research. Also reads the critics: Gebru on AI hype, Gary Marcus on capability claims, Kate Crawford on power concentration. Students are expected to form defensible views and argue for them. The goal is a student who can engage with METR researchers or AI Now researchers with equal fluency — who has read both literatures and can say something substantive about where they agree, where they conflict, and what governance implications follow from each.
### Track B: Operations, Programs, and Communications
_Targets: GovAI Applied Track, CAIS Program Manager, PAI program operations, AI safety org communications_

**AIHUMMA-B1 · Program Design and Convening (3 credits)** How do you design a fellowship? A workshop? A convening that produces something? This course treats program design as an intellectual craft, drawing on literatures from organizational sociology, adult learning theory, and the history of influential knowledge-building institutions (the Asilomar conference, the Santa Fe Institute, Pugwash). Students design a real program — a fellowship selection process, a three-day workshop curriculum, a field-building initiative — and defend it.

**AIHUMMA-B2 · Strategic Communications for AI Governance (3 credits)** Not PR — strategic communication. How do you translate between technical communities and policy communities? How do you build an organization's public presence and narrative? What makes a communications strategy in this space different from corporate comms? Students study real cases: how CAIS has positioned itself, how GovAI builds its public profile, how AI Now uses media strategically as part of advocacy. Assignments include a communications audit of a real org, and a strategic communications plan for a fictional new AI governance nonprofit.

**AIHUMMA-B3 · Fundraising, Operations, and Field-Building (3 credits)** The unsexy course that makes everything else possible. How do AI safety and governance organizations get funded? (Open Philanthropy, Survival and Flourishing Fund, Longview Philanthropy, EA-adjacent funders, plus foundations like MacArthur and Hewlett.) How do you write a grant proposal? How do you design an operations infrastructure for a small, mission-driven org? This is the course that trains CAIS Operations Associates and PAI program staff — people who know how the field's institutional infrastructure works and can build more of it.
## Practicum and Capstone (9 credits)
### AIHUMMA-P1 · Field Practicum (3 credits)
A structured 8-week engagement with a partner organization — ideally one of your five anchor orgs or an adjacent body. Students work on a real project: a policy memo series, a fellowship cohort design, a communications campaign, a research synthesis. GovEx's existing relationships are an asset here — government and city-level AI bodies would be natural partners alongside the NGO side.
### AIHUMMA-P2 · Capstone Seminar (3 credits)
Students develop their capstone project with peer critique and faculty mentorship across a semester. The seminar meets weekly to workshop drafts and hear from practitioners. Every session includes one practitioner guest — someone actually working in one of the target orgs — who responds to student work in progress. This creates a network effect: students leave having had real intellectual exchanges with people who might later hire them.
### AIHUMMA-P3 · Capstone Project (3 credits)
A substantive deliverable in one of the following formats — student chooses based on track and placement target:
- A **policy research report** of GovAI white-paper quality (15-25 pages), submitted to a real organization or published as a working paper
- A **program design document** — a complete blueprint for a fellowship, workshop series, or field-building initiative, with budget, selection criteria, curriculum, and theory of change
- A **fellowship application portfolio** — a GovAI Applied Track or Berkman Klein application, developed to submission-ready quality, with accompanying research proposal and writing samples
- A **strategic communications plan** for a real or fictional AI governance org, with sample outputs across multiple genres
