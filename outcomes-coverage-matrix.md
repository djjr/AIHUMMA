# AIHUMMA Outcomes Coverage Matrix
## JHU AI Course Inventory — Synthesis Analysis

*Based on outcomes tagging of 65 JHU AI-related courses across 9 schools*
*Refined after manual review of CH-04 (cultural forms) and addition of HI-02, SD-01, SD-04, SD-07*
*Generated for comparison with AIHUMMA program design*

---

## How to Read This Document

Each outcome code was assigned to courses where that skill is plausibly developed — explicitly stated or reasonably inferred. The counts show how many of the 65 courses address each outcome. High counts mean the JHU curriculum covers it broadly; low counts identify **gaps** — outcomes that, if central to AIHUMMA, will need to be built rather than assumed from existing courses.

The **strategic implication for AIHUMMA** inverts the intuition: outcomes *well-covered* in existing JHU courses are ones AIHUMMA students could potentially satisfy through electives. Outcomes *sparsely covered* are the ones AIHUMMA must own and build distinctively.

### Cluster CH: Critical & Humanistic Analysis

| Code | Description | Courses | % | Assessment |
|------|-------------|---------|---|------------|
| CH-05 | Evaluate claims about AI consciousness/intelligence | 45 | 69% | ✅ Very well-covered — a baseline assumption |
| CH-02 | Analyze AI discourse as rhetorical construct | 21 | 32% | ✅ Moderate-good |
| CH-04 | Critique AI in cultural forms (film, theatre, fiction) | 16 | 25% | ✅ Appropriately targeted (16 courses after refinement) |
| CH-01 | Situate AI in intellectual/political history | 11 | 17% | ⚠️ Moderate — important for AIHUMMA foundation |
| CH-06 | Analyze AI through feminist/postcolonial/critical race lens | 6 | 9% | ⚠️ Sparse — important for equity framing |
| CH-03 | Apply humanistic methods (ethnography, close reading) to AI | 4 | 6% | 🔴 Rare — AIHUMMA must own this explicitly |

**AIHUMMA note:** CH-05 (questions of AI consciousness and intelligence) is nearly universal — this is the philosophical water the whole curriculum swims in. AIHUMMA does not need to build this; it can be assumed as background. The genuine gap is CH-03: rigorous deployment of humanistic *methods* as analytical tools for studying AI phenomena. Only four courses in the catalog teach ethnographic, discourse-analytic, or close-reading approaches applied specifically to AI. This is a key AIHUMMA differentiator. CH-04 now sits at a realistic 25% — the courses that actually engage with AI in film, theatre, and fiction rather than mentioning societal implications broadly.

### Cluster EV: Ethics & Values

| Code | Description | Courses | % | Assessment |
|------|-------------|---------|---|------------|
| EV-01 | Apply ethical frameworks to AI decisions | 24 | 37% | ✅ Well-covered |
| EV-02 | Identify and analyze algorithmic bias | 12 | 18% | ✅ Moderate |
| EV-04 | Analyze ethics in high-stakes domains | 12 | 18% | ✅ Moderate |
| EV-03 | Evaluate responsible AI instruments | 10 | 15% | ⚠️ Moderate |
| EV-06 | Assess privacy/consent/data rights implications | 5 | 8% | ⚠️ Sparse |
| EV-05 | Distinguish procedural vs. structural ethics critique | 1 | 2% | 🔴 Nearly absent — AIHUMMA must own this |

**AIHUMMA note:** EV-05 is the sharpest ethics gap in the entire taxonomy. Only one course explicitly addresses the difference between procedural ethics (impact assessments, checklists) and structural critique (the system is the problem, ethics discourse is being weaponized). This is the Timnit Gebru / AI Now critique — "ethics washing." A humanities-trained policy student who can make this distinction fluently is doing something very different from one who learned to fill out an ethics checklist. AIHUMMA's F3 course should make this a central, named learning outcome.

| RM-01 | Design/conduct qualitative research (ethnography, interviewing) | 1 | 2% | 🔴 Nearly absent |

**AIHUMMA note:** RM-01 is the sharpest methods gap in the entire catalog. One course — LAMA Lab — teaches ethnographic and qualitative methods applied to AI. Yet qualitative research (elite interviewing, document analysis, process tracing) is exactly the methodology GovAI and CSET use to produce policy-relevant research. JHU is an engineering-heavy research university and qualitative social science methods are not well-represented in its AI curriculum. AIHUMMA's Research Methods course (A2) fills an almost empty niche.

| CW-05 | Use GenAI to enhance writing/communication workflows | 0 | 0% | 🔴 **Absent** |

**AIHUMMA note:** CW is the most uniformly sparse cluster in the catalog. Writing instruction — particularly *genre fluency* across policy writing formats — is almost entirely absent from JHU's AI curriculum. The one journalism course (Storytelling with Data and AI) teaches data narrative, but policy memo writing, white paper production, and the genre conventions of AI governance writing are taught nowhere. AIHUMMA-F4 (Writing for Policy) is not redundant with anything that exists. CW-05 (using GenAI to enhance one's own research and writing practice) is also absent, despite being a practically critical skill for every target job role.
