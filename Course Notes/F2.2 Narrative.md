*The purpose of the course is to help students acquire a [[F2.1 Conceptual Repertoire|conceptual repertoire]], a sense of the [[F2.2 Narrative|historical narrative]] which gives real world meaning to these concepts, and a preliminary appreciation of [[F2.3 From Repertoire to Practice|what it takes to realize concepts in real world institutions]].*

*The following paragraphs are a sketch of **the narrative**. The goal is for students to develop habits of thought which can balance drawing on historical analogy and the recognition of novelty so they can use historical cases with enough analytical precision to know where they illuminate and where they break down, where the received conceptual apparatus fits and where it struggles.*

----
Governance is not a new idea. Humans have always had to solve coordination problems, manage shared resources, adjudicate disputes, and constrain dangerous practices. The Alpine meadow commons that Ostrom studied, the Roman aqueduct office, the guild regulations of medieval towns — these are not primitive anticipations of modern governance; they are governance, functioning on the scale and at the speed that pre-industrial social life required. Modernity and industrialization did not change the need for governance but its conditions: the scale of potential harm, the speed of change, the anonymity of social relationships, and the technical opacity of the things being governed. A village could enforce norms about how many animals grazed on common land because everyone could see the animals and knew the herders. But it cannot regulate a factory's effluent, a railway's boiler pressure, or a pharmaceutical's chemical composition by the same means. The institutions of the regulatory state, the inspectorate, the licensing agency, the standards body, the expert bureau are the answer to the question: how do you govern at scale, at speed, across social distance, and through technical complexity? It is not an obvious answer. It was invented, painfully and slowly, often in response to disasters that made visible the cost of not governing.

That is, the history of technology governance is largely a history of catastrophe followed by institutional improvisation. The pattern recurs: a technology develops faster than existing legal categories can accommodate; harms accumulate that are real but not yet legible as harms requiring governance; a disaster makes the accumulated harm suddenly visible and politically intolerable; institutions are created or reformed; those institutions are then slowly captured by the interests they regulate, and/or outpaced by the next generation of the technology; and the cycle begins again. Thalidomide produced pre-market pharmaceutical approval. The Triangle Shirtwaist fire produced workplace safety law. Minamata disease produced environmental regulation. The 2008 financial crisis produced stress testing and systemic risk frameworks. The 737 MAX produced a reckoning with self-certification. No particular institutional response was inevitable; each was contested, partial, and inadequate. International governance follows the same pattern: the IAEA was created after Hiroshima demonstrated the stakes, the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty came twelve years after that, and the verification regime remains imperfect. The Asilomar moratorium on recombinant DNA research lasted roughly two years before competitive pressures dissolved it. Civilization does not stumble gracefully toward governance of technology. It just stumbles.

AI is both continuous and discontinuous with what came before. The continuities include dual-use capability that outpaces regulatory categories, information asymmetry between developers and overseers, collective action problems in a competitive landscape, the jurisdictional gap between national authority and global technology, the verification problem of governing systems whose internal workings resist legible inspection. A student who understands the IAEA's approach to verification is not starting from scratch on AI evaluation; a student who can explain the FDA's pre-market approval logic is not starting from scratch on foundation model governance. The historical analogies are analytically productive, not merely illustrative. But the discontinuities are also important. The pace of capability development is more acute than in previous cases. The opacity of large neural systems is more fundamental than the complexity of previous technologies; they are not merely difficult for outsiders to understand, but also resistant to legible inspection even by those who built them. The concentration of capability in a very small number of private actors, without the physical infrastructure constraints that limited nuclear or pharmaceutical proliferation, creates governance problems for which the existing institutional repertoire offers partial but not complete answers. And the prospect of systems that act as agents or that recursively modify their own capabilities introduces a category of risk for which the historical record offers analogy but not precedent.
