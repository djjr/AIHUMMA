The purpose of the course is to help students acquire a [[F2.1 Conceptual Repertoire|conceptual repertoire]], a sense of the [[F2.2 Narrative|historical narrative]] which gives real world meaning to these concepts, and a preliminary appreciation of [[F2.3 From Repertoire to Practice|what it takes to realize concepts in real world institutions]].

----
Knowing concepts and knowing history are necessary but not sufficient. A student who can define regulatory capture, trace the development of the IAEA verification regime, and explain why the Collingridge dilemma makes anticipatory governance difficult is a student with genuine analytical preparation, but not yet a practitioner. This course does not close the distance between these entirely, but it lays the foundation by maintaining a focus on knowledge use.

Practitioners, unlike academicians, reason under constraint toward a decision. Academic analysis is properly open-ended: the right conclusion is whatever the evidence and argument support, and "this is more complicated than it appears" is a legitimate answer. For practitioners, it is not. They work inside institutions, under time pressure, with incomplete information, toward outputs that must be legible to audiences with different training and different interests and the outputs must recommend something. The governance analogy memo that anchors this course is designed to practice exactly that discipline: not "here are several frameworks that might apply," but "here is the framework I am arguing for, here is why I am arguing for it, and here is where it breaks down." The last clause is not hedge, but analytical honesty.

This course begins building three practitioner capacities that the rest of the curriculum will develop further. The first is _genre fluency_: policy writing as a distinct literary form with its own conventions, constraints, and standards of evidence. The second is _institutional imagination_: the ability to think not only about what should be done but about what kind of institution could do it, under what authority, with what resources, facing what resistance, and with what likelihood of capture or decay over time. The third is _calibrated analogical reasoning_: using historical cases not as rhetorical decoration but as analytical instruments, pressing an analogy until it breaks and treating the breaking point as information rather than embarrassment.
