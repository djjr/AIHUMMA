### Bootcamp 0
A Few Things to Know About Before We Get Started
1. AI systems automate by seeking goals, not by following instructions. Traditional software does what a programmer explicitly told it to do. AI systems are given an objective and learn from data how to pursue it. The simplest version of this is a thermostat: it doesn't follow a script, it pursues a target state. Understanding this distinction explains why AI systems fail in unexpected ways and why governing them requires different tools than governing conventional software.

**2. An AI model is a function: it takes inputs and produces outputs.** A model is not a database, a search engine, or a decision-maker. It is a mathematical function — trained to map inputs to outputs in ways that were useful during training. Understanding a model means understanding what inputs it accepts, what outputs it produces, and what it was trained to optimize.

**3. AI systems perform five basic operations: classification, prediction, optimization, generation, and translation.** Classification assigns inputs to categories. Prediction estimates future values from past patterns. Optimization finds the best action given a set of constraints and objectives. Generation produces new content from learned patterns. Translation converts information from one form or representation to another. Most real AI systems combine several of these.

**4. The training data is the origin of the system's behavior.** An AI system does not follow rules its designers wrote. It does what its training data implied it should do. This means bias, unexpected failures, and harmful outputs are not bugs in the conventional sense — they are the system working as designed on data that contained problems. A system trained on confounded data learns the confound.

**5. Training, evaluation, and deployment are distinct moments with distinct stakes.** A system is trained on one set of data, evaluated on another, and then deployed into a world that may resemble neither. Strong performance during evaluation does not guarantee strong performance in deployment. Many governance problems live in the gap between evaluation conditions and real-world conditions.

**6. Everything an AI system processes is a number, and everything it produces is a number too.** Words, images, sounds, and decisions are all converted into numerical representations before a model touches them. The model performs mathematical transformations on those numbers and produces more numbers, which are then converted back into words, images, or decisions. This is not a metaphor — it is literally what is happening. It means that text, images, and audio can all be handled by the same underlying architecture, that "meaning" for these systems is a matter of mathematical proximity, and that the function in concept 2 is arithmetic at scale.

**7. "Learning," "understanding," and "reasoning" are metaphors, not descriptions.** These systems identify statistical patterns in data. They do not understand in the way humans understand. Using cognitive language about AI systems is not wrong, but it is always analogical — and when the analogy is forgotten, it produces bad policy.

**8. The shift from narrow AI to general-purpose systems changed the governance problem fundamentally.** Earlier AI systems did one thing. Current foundation models do many things, often in ways their developers did not anticipate. A system that can generate text, write code, answer questions, and take actions in the world is not a bigger version of a spam filter. It is a different kind of governance problem.
