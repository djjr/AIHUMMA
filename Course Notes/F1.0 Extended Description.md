### Description
The purpose of this course is to provide students with the technical foundations necessary to engage with AI as an object of policy analysis and critical thinking. We accomplish this by striking a balance between presenting axiomatic, hands-on, and motivated knowledge.  

The course alternates between technical bootcamps and close engagement with real governance artifacts: model cards, capability evaluation reports, red-teaming assessments, and the public documentation that AI laboratories release about their own systems. Bootcamps introduce the concepts (classification, training and fine-tuning, evaluation, deployment) that the artifacts raise but don't explain. Artifacts introduce the questions; bootcamps answer them. The dialectical interplay reveals how learning them critically is a form of technical competence, and building technical know-how is a form of critical competence.

By the end of the course, students will be able to use terms like "RLHF," "benchmark evaluation," and "alignment" correctly in policy writing; read a capability evaluation and identify what it is and is not claiming; and recognize when technical language in a policy document is overreaching or underselling. They will have begun to engage with AI researchers and engineers as informed interlocutors rather than outsiders using buzzwords.

No prior technical background is assumed or required.

#### Pedagogy
Active learning: chunking; prior associations; appropriate examples; foundational materials first ;connections with different contexts; interleaving; dual coding; meaningful milestones.
