**3 credits | Semester 1**

Governing AI is not a new human problem; it is a new instance of the recurring problem of how to live with and manage powerful, disruptive, dual-use, rapidly evolving technologies. This course leverages students' prior training in humanities and social sciences as it treats AI governance as a problem in political economy, cultural adaptation, and institutional design, anchored in comparative case study: books, railways, nuclear inspection, pharmaceutical approval, financial regulation, aviation safety, and biotech moratoriums. Students who complete the course arrive at AI governance conversations already knowing the language — regulation, institutions, the IAEA, regulatory capture, commons, governance. Signature assignment: a governance analogy memo.
