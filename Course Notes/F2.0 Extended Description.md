**F2 Institutions, Governance, and the Regulation of Risky Technologies 3 credits | Semester 1**
###### Description
Governing AI is not a new human problem; it is a new instance of the recurring problem of how to live with and manage powerful, disruptive, dual-use, rapidly evolving technologies. This course leverages students' prior training in humanities and social sciences as it treats AI governance as a problem in political economy, cultural adaptation, and institutional design, anchored in comparative case study: books, railways, nuclear inspection, pharmaceutical approval, financial regulation, aviation safety, and biotech moratoriums. Students who complete the course arrive at AI governance conversations already knowing the language — regulation, institutions, the IAEA, regulatory capture, commons, governance. Signature assignment: a governance analogy memo.
###### Approach
This course starts by drawing on humanities and social sciences "classics" as on-ramps by validating prior training _and_ introducing the analytical vocabulary the rest of the course will use (verification, dual-use, institutional form, regulatory capture, etc.). We examining two classic cases students will be directly or indirectly familiar with. Examples of case studies:

- We read _Frankenstein (1818)_ as a story of governance failure. Victor's crime isn't creating the creature but abandoning it, refusing to accept the obligations that come with novel power. We analogize this with the AI liability/accountability debate (who is responsible for downstream harms from a deployed system?) and onto the broader question of creator responsibility that runs through biosafety and pharmaceutical regulation. The running problem: an invention isn't intrinsically dangerous, it becomes dangerous through institutional neglect. 
- Every student knows about Gutenberg, but few know that the English Crown's response was the Stationers' Company (1557), a licensing regime granting a guild monopoly on printing in exchange for suppressing seditious material. It's a pre-market approval analog, a dual-use governance case (same press, Bible and pamphlet), and an early instance of regulatory capture (the regulated industry administering its own regulation). But the Stationers' Company was a national regulatory regime (cf. EU AI act or US executive orders) and printing very quickly became a cross-border phenomenon.  The Catholic Church faced the same problem and its Index Librorum Prohibitorum in (1559) was an attempt to solve the international coordination problem that no single sovereign could solve alone. The motivations behind the Index, the institutions built to enforce it, and its eventual failure are fertile material for the course.
- In 186 BCE the Roman Senate was alarmed by the spread of Bacchic mystery cults. It issued a decree, _Senatus Consultum de Bacchanalibus_, requiring licensing and registration, capping meeting sizes, mandating prior approval for any gathering above a small threshold, and banning the cults' organizational infrastructure. A practice with genuine social value (religious community) was perceived as a politically threatening coordination mechanism; the state responds with a registration/prior-authorization regime, a structure that recurs in AI governance debates about, say, foundation model access.  
- A second Roman case is the _Roman cura aquarum_ (the office overseeing aqueduct management). Here we have a case about public goods, infrastructure governance, and the problem of distributed benefit versus concentrated risk. Frontinus's [_De Aquaeductu_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_aquaeductu) is actually a governance document, not just a technical manual. 
- Hardin's _Tragedy of the Commons_ (1968) and Ostrom's response is canonical social science that students will likely be familiar with.  These works introduce the conceptual vocabulary of collective action problems, commons governance, and polycentric regulation, all live concepts in AI governance (AI as a commons, race-to-the-bottom dynamics in capability development, self-governance vs. mandatory regimes). One useful meta-lesson here is that it's a case where the dominant theoretical framework (Hardin) turned out to be empirically wrong that commons lead to tragedy unless privatized or strongly regulated. Ostrom (1990) showed that many commons (e.g., fisheries in Maine and Turkey, irrigation systems in Spain and the Philippines, Alpine meadows in Switzerland, forests in Japan) had been managed sustainably for centuries or millennia without either privatization or state coercion.
- Railways were the first technology that was simultaneously: economically transformative, inherently dangerous, cross-jurisdictional, and fast-moving enough to outpace existing legal categories. Railways changed our sense of time, space, community, economy, and law.  The regulatory response invented many now taken-for-granted institutional forms such as mandatory accident investigations (precursor to the NTSB), common carrier obligations (non-discrimination in access), and nationalization as a governance tool. A governance failure mode rooted in the dual mandate to promote and regulate (cf. FAA) appears early. Every student who has taken economic history knows railways; few know the institutional response in any detail. The _Railway Acts_ (especially [Gladstone's 1844 Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Railway_Regulation_Act_1844)) provide possible text sources. See also: Bailey, Mark F. 2004.  [THE 1844 RAILWAY ACT : A VIOLATION OF LAISSEZ-FAIRE POLITICAL ECONOMY?](https://www.jstor.org/stable/23723122) History of Economic Ideas Vol. 12, No. 3, pp. 7-24.

These starting points means prior training gets explicitly _leveraged_ rather than treated as background noise. The course treats AI governance as a problem in political economy, social control, cultural evolution, and institutional design, using comparison cases from other high-risk technology governance domains. 

**The central argument of the course:** AI governance isn't new; it's a new instance of recurring problems in governing powerful, dual-use, rapidly evolving technologies. Humans have done this before, most recently, imperfectly, with nuclear weapons, pharmaceuticals, financial instruments, aviation, and biosafety. What did we learn? What failed? What's actually new about AI?

**Case studies to anchor the course:**

- Nuclear: international inspection regimes, the IAEA, how verification works when you can't see inside the weapons
- Pharma: the FDA's emergence from disaster (thalidomide), pre-market approval, what "clinical trial" means as an institutional form
- Finance: post-2008 stress testing, the FSOC, systemic risk as a governance concept
- Aviation: the FAA's dual mandate (promote and regulate), what went wrong with 737 MAX, the limits of self-certification
- Biosafety: dual-use research of concern, the Asilomar conference, voluntary vs. mandatory moratoriums
- Pollution: air and water quality regulation ranges from automobile smog checks to factory emission control with an immense institutional infrastructure to generate socially desirable outcomes
Approach to case studies. What set of generic ideas about governance and institutions and regulation and technology does the case illustrate? What is transferrable? We get some of this from case-to-case comparison and we get some by analogizing to AI. This latter should build on scaffolding material in AIHUMMA-F1 [[F1 How AI Systems Work]].

**Why this matters for our curriculum targets:** Organizations like GovAI, CAIS, and AI Now all produce work that draws on these analogies. A student who knows the IAEA inspections regime in detail is already fluent in a conversation that dominates technical AI governance. Our students arrive at GovAI already knowing the language.

**Signature assignment:** A "governance analogy memo" in which the writer identifies a prior regulatory regime that offers the best model for governing a specific AI risk (e.g., foundation model evaluation), arguing for their choice, and explaining where the analogy breaks down.
