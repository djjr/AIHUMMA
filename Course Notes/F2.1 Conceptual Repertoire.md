The purpose of the course is to help students acquire a [[F2.1 Conceptual Repertoire|conceptual repertoire]], a sense of the [[F2.2 Narrative|historical narrative]] which gives real world meaning to these concepts, and a preliminary appreciation of [[F2.3 From Repertoire to Practice|what it takes to realize concepts in real world institutions]].
### I. What Governance Is Trying To Do

_The problem-definition layer — why governance exists at all, and how the problem gets constructed_

**Externalities** — costs or benefits that fall on parties outside a transaction; the basic market-failure rationale for regulation. When a factory pollutes a river it doesn't own, the cost is real but doesn't appear in the factory's accounts. Governance is one mechanism for making those costs legible and assignable.

**Public goods** — resources that are non-excludable (you can't easily prevent anyone from using them) and non-rivalrous (one person's use doesn't diminish another's). Clean air, open research, safety standards. The commons problem in its most fundamental form.

**Systemic risk** — the possibility that failure in one node propagates across an entire interconnected system, producing harms far exceeding the original failure. Distinct from individual risk. Post-2008 finance gave us the sharpest recent vocabulary for this; AI infrastructure creates analogous dependencies.

**Catastrophic and irreversible harm** — the special governance problem posed by harms that cannot be undone after the fact. Standard regulatory logic assumes you can correct errors; catastrophic risk requires anticipatory governance because there is no corrective iteration.

**Dual-use** — the same technology, knowledge, or capability that produces benefit can produce harm; the printing press prints Bibles and seditious pamphlets; the same biological technique that enables vaccines enables weaponization. This is not an edge case. It is the normal condition of powerful technologies, and it is why governance of technology is structurally harder than governance of purely harmful things.

**General-purpose technology** — technologies that transform multiple sectors simultaneously (steam, electricity, computing, AI), making targeted single-sector regulation structurally inadequate. The regulatory categories built around one application don't fit the others.

**Information asymmetry** — the regulated party knows more about the technology than the regulator; the foundational epistemic problem of all technical regulation. Humanists who have worked with archives will recognize the power dynamics of controlling what the assessing party can see. The governance problem of information asymmetry is structurally similar: the powerful party controls access to the evidence.

**The pacing problem** — technology develops faster than the institutional capacity to evaluate and govern it. The Index Librorum Prohibitorum couldn't keep up with the printing rate; AI evaluation frameworks face the same lag. Governance institutions are always, to some degree, governing last year's technology.

**The construction of the problem** — before governance can happen, a situation must be defined as a problem requiring governance. Who does that defining, through what processes, using what categories, is itself a political and cultural act. Historians of science call this the social construction of risk; rhetoricians call it framing; Foucault called it problematization. Whatever the vocabulary, it belongs at the front of any serious governance analysis — and humanities scholars trained in the history of concepts are unusually well-positioned to do this work.
### II. The Institutional Toolkit

_The forms governance takes — what regulators actually build_

**Licensing and registration regimes** — prior authorization as a governance form; who gets to operate, and under what conditions. The Stationers' Company (1557) is an early instance. Foundation model access proposals are a current one. The form is ancient; the application is new.

**Pre-market approval** — the requirement to demonstrate safety before deployment, rather than correcting harms after the fact. The FDA model, born from the thalidomide disaster. The implicit theory: some harms are too severe to permit a trial-and-error approach.

**Mandatory disclosure and reporting** — reducing information asymmetry through required transparency: incident reporting, emissions data, clinical trial registration. Governance through making things visible that would otherwise remain hidden.

**Inspection and verification regimes** — how you monitor compliance when you cannot directly observe the thing you are governing. The IAEA cannot look inside warheads; it infers from what it can see. AI auditing faces an analogous opacity. The verification problem is as much epistemological as technical.

**Accident investigation** — the institutional form for learning from failure. Mandatory, independent, and designed to be non-punitive so that information flows rather than being suppressed. The NTSB model. The premise: failure is a source of knowledge, but only if the investigation process is insulated from liability pressures.

**Common carrier obligations** — non-discrimination in access to essential infrastructure. The governance of chokepoints: railroads, telephone networks, and potentially AI infrastructure.

**Graduated sanctions** — proportionate response to violation. The alternative to zero-tolerance regimes that are formally strict but credibly unenforced because the penalty is disproportionate to the infraction. Ostrom identified this as a design feature of commons governance arrangements that survive.

**Self-certification and self-regulation** — delegation of compliance verification to the regulated party. Sometimes an efficient use of expertise; more often a failure-mode generator. The 737 MAX is the paradigm case of what happens when the certifier and the developer are functionally the same entity.

**Voluntary moratoriums** — self-imposed restraint without legal compulsion. The Asilomar conference on recombinant DNA (1975) is the canonical case: scientists agreeing to pause research until safety frameworks could be established. Voluntary restraint raises the same collective-action problem as any commons: it works until defection becomes individually rational.

**Nationalization** — public ownership as a governance instrument. What it means to decide that an infrastructure is too consequential to leave to private management. Not a relic of the past; contemporary debates about AI infrastructure invoke it.

**Canon and precedent as governance** — not only legal precedent but the broader phenomenon of authoritative cases and texts that structure subsequent argument. The governance analogy memo this course produces is an exercise in working with precedent: selecting, applying, and contesting the authority of prior cases. Humanities scholars who have spent years studying how canons form, how they are contested, and how they are selectively invoked are already practicing a cognate analytical skill.
### III. Structural Problems in Governing Technology

_The recurring difficulties that appear across every case — the grammar of governance failure_

**The jurisdiction gap** — national regulatory regimes attempting to govern cross-border technologies. The Stationers' Company could suppress printing in London; it could not suppress printing in Amsterdam. The EU AI Act faces a version of the same problem. Regulatory authority and technological reach rarely coincide.

**The dual mandate** — the same agency charged with promoting an industry and regulating it. A structural conflict of interest baked into institutional design. The FAA promoted aviation and regulated it; the tension eventually contributed to the 737 MAX failures. The same structure appears in less obvious forms: the academic department that both trains and evaluates its own students; the church that both administers sacraments and adjudicates heresy. The structural problem is identical across these cases; the governance stakes differ in scale.

**The evaluation lag** — by the time assessment of a technology is complete, the technology has moved. The Index couldn't condemn books faster than presses could produce them. AI evaluation frameworks face the same asymmetry between the pace of capability development and the pace of institutional assessment.

**The verification problem** — how you confirm compliance with things you cannot directly observe. The IAEA's central challenge is inferring the state of weapons programs from what it is permitted to see. AI interpretability is the current version: governance requires understanding what a system is doing, but the systems resist legible inspection.

**The attribution problem** — when harm occurs, who is responsible? Especially acute for systems with long causal chains and distributed agency. Financial contagion, AI downstream harms. This is also a problem historians recognize: attributing causation across complex, multi-actor sequences is exactly what historical analysis does. The legal-governance concept of attribution and the historian's concept of causation are in close dialogue.

**The Collingridge dilemma** — a technology's impacts are unknowable until it is widely adopted, but controlling it becomes harder once it is. Governance must act under uncertainty early, or accept constraint later. Historians will recognize a temporal analog: the significance of a turning point is only visible retrospectively, by which time the options available at the turning point have closed. The retrospective clarity that makes historical analysis satisfying is precisely what governance in real time lacks.

**Path dependence** — early institutional choices constrain later options; regulatory structures outlive the conditions that produced them. The categories created by early governance decisions shape what can subsequently be thought and done. Historians of institutions and literary scholars trained in the history of genre will recognize this: forms persist beyond the circumstances that produced them, and they do work on what comes after.

**Precedent and institutional memory** — governance institutions inherit the choices of their predecessors and are often more constrained by those inheritances than by present conditions. This is path dependence experienced from the inside: not as an abstract structural feature but as the felt weight of prior decisions, which is how historians and legal scholars encounter it.
### IV. Political Economy — The Forces That Shape Governance

_Why governance doesn't simply optimize for public welfare_

**Regulatory capture** — the regulated industry gains disproportionate influence over its regulator. The Stationers' Company is an early and unusually pure case: the regulated party administered the regulation. The revolving door between industry and agency is the more familiar contemporary form.

**Institutional capture** — a regulatory apparatus is used to advance the interests of the administering institution rather than the stated public purpose. The Index Librorum was formally a safety regime; it also functioned as an instrument of theological enforcement and competitive suppression. Galileo's condemnation is the most famous case. This is not industry-capture; it is the state or institution using safety mechanisms to advance its own interests. Both forms exist; distinguishing them matters for diagnosis.

**Narrative and legitimation** — governance arrangements don't only regulate behavior; they tell stories about why the regulation is legitimate, who counts as a threat, what counts as safety. The Index narrated certain knowledge as dangerous and certain readers as imperiled; that narrative did governance work independent of any enforcement mechanism. Regulatory debates are always partly struggles over whose story prevails and whose categories organize the field. Humanities scholars trained to read these narratives as constructions rather than descriptions are doing analysis that political economists often miss.

**Rent-seeking** — using regulatory processes to entrench market position rather than to advance safety. Incumbents often support regulatory regimes that impose compliance costs they can absorb but competitors cannot. Regulation as competitive strategy.

**Concentrated benefits, diffuse costs** — regulated industries typically have stronger, more focused incentives to shape governance than the general public has to resist it. The asymmetry of stakes produces asymmetric political mobilization. The classic explanation for why capture is so common.

**Veto players** — actors whose consent is required for institutional change. The more veto players in a system, the harder reform becomes even when failure is obvious and widely recognized. Federalism, bicameralism, international sovereignty: each adds veto players.

**Legitimacy** — the perceived right to govern; the condition under which compliance is secured without continuous enforcement. Weber formalized the concept, but historians, literary scholars, and anthropologists have been studying it under other names — authority, consent, hegemony, symbolic power — for longer. Gramsci's hegemony and Bourdieu's symbolic capital are cognate concepts that humanities scholars will recognize as doing related analytical work.

**The constitutive power of categories** — regulatory regimes don't only govern things that already exist; they bring categories into being that shape what gets made and how. The FDA's definition of a "drug" determined what could be sold, by whom, and under what conditions. The EU AI Act's definition of an "AI system" will do similar work. Humanities scholars trained in the history of concepts — Foucault's genealogies, the construction of race as a category, the emergence of "literature" as a disciplinary object — are well-positioned to analyze how regulatory categories do political and cultural work, often without announcing that they are doing it.

**Voice and representation** — who gets to speak in a regulatory process, whose testimony counts as evidence, whose experience is legible as data. Notice-and-comment rulemaking, congressional testimony, expert witness standards: these are all regimes for managing whose voice enters governance. Humanities scholars trained in questions of whose stories get told and whose don't are directly prepared for this analysis — and it is not peripheral to governance, it is constitutive of it.

**International regulatory competition** — when multiple jurisdictions compete for industry on regulatory terms, the result can be a race to the bottom. Regulatory stringency becomes a competitive disadvantage if the regulated activity can relocate. The printing-in-Amsterdam problem scaled to global capital mobility.
### V. Collective Action and Commons

_The game-theoretic substrate_

**The collective action problem** — individually rational behavior producing collectively irrational outcomes. Hardin's tragedy of the commons is the paradigm case; arms races, pollution, and AI capability races are others. The problem appears wherever what is good for each actor considered separately is bad for all actors considered together.

**The prisoner's dilemma structure** — why voluntary restraint fails when defection is individually rational and the other party's choices are uncertain. The logic underlying the case for mandatory rather than voluntary governance regimes. Also the logic that makes international coordination so difficult.

**Polycentric governance** — Ostrom's alternative to Hardin's privatize-or-coerce binary. Governance emerging from nested, overlapping institutional arrangements at multiple scales, rather than from a single sovereign authority. The empirical finding that many commons govern themselves successfully through community-developed rules challenges the assumption that only hierarchy or markets can solve collective action problems.

**Ostrom's design principles** — the empirical features of commons governance arrangements that persist: defined boundaries, locally adapted rules, collective choice arrangements, monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict resolution mechanisms, recognized rights to organize, and nested governance for larger systems. A diagnostic checklist for evaluating proposed governance arrangements. Not a guarantee of success but a vocabulary for asking why arrangements work or fail.

**Race-to-the-bottom dynamics** — when competitive pressure drives standards downward. The regulatory haven problem at scale: if compliance is costly and relocation is possible, stringent jurisdictions export both activity and harm.

**Coordination problems** — cases where everyone would prefer a common standard to no standard, but disagrees on which standard. Distinct from the prisoner's dilemma: interests are complementary rather than conflicting, but agreement is still difficult. Common weights and measures, technical standards, safety protocols.
### VI. International and Multilateral Governance

_The extra layer of difficulty when technology crosses borders_

**Treaty regimes** — the basic institutional form of international governance. What they can and cannot enforce; the gap between treaty commitments and treaty compliance; the role of verification in sustaining them.

**Non-proliferation logic** — controlling access to dangerous capabilities through export controls, inspection regimes, and technology transfer restrictions. The IAEA as paradigm. The current debates about AI chip export controls are direct applications.

**Verification under sovereignty constraints** — inspection is possible only with permission; permission is not always granted; what you can infer from permitted inspection is always partial. The fundamental tension between international governance and state sovereignty.

**Harmonization vs. mutual recognition** — two strategies for managing regulatory divergence across jurisdictions. Harmonization (agree on common standards) is more robust but harder to achieve; mutual recognition (accept each other's standards as equivalent) is more achievable but creates pressure toward the weaker standard.

**Regulatory havens** — jurisdictions that attract activity precisely by offering lighter governance. The printing-in-Amsterdam problem recurs across technology domains. The existence of havens undermines the effectiveness of stringent domestic regimes.

**The weakest-link problem** — international regimes are only as strong as the least-compliant significant actor. A biosafety regime that excludes major research powers is not a biosafety regime; it is a coordination among the already-cautious.
### VII. Risk and Precaution

_The conceptual vocabulary for reasoning about harm under uncertainty_

**Tail risk** — low-probability, high-consequence events. Expected-value reasoning systematically underweights them because the probability is small even when the consequence is enormous. Catastrophic risk governance requires different decision frameworks than standard cost-benefit analysis.

**The precautionary principle** — act to prevent potential harm even under uncertainty; don't wait for proof of harm before governing. Its versions, its limits, and its political uses. Both a genuine decision rule and a rhetorical instrument; distinguishing the two is an analytical task.

**Known unknowns and unknown unknowns** — a genuine epistemological distinction with governance implications. Known unknowns (we know what we don't know) can in principle be governed; unknown unknowns (we don't know what we don't know) cannot. Much of what makes powerful technologies hard to govern is that their failure modes are not legible in advance.

**Risk-benefit tradeoffs** — the unavoidability of trading off safety against other values. Governance does not eliminate risk; it manages the distribution of risk and the assignment of its costs. Who bears the risk of a technology and who captures its benefits is a political question dressed as a technical one.

**Strict liability vs. negligence** — two theories of who bears the cost of harm. Strict liability assigns cost to the producer regardless of care taken; negligence assigns cost only when reasonable care was not taken. The choice between them produces different incentive structures for precaution and different distributional outcomes.

**Moral hazard** — when protection from consequences reduces incentives to avoid the risks that generate them. The too-big-to-fail problem in finance: if you know you will be rescued, you take risks you wouldn't otherwise take. Applies wherever insurance, indemnification, or bail-out expectations exist.
### VIII. Accountability and Responsibility

_The normative layer — who owes what to whom, and how that gets established_

**Creator responsibility** — Frankenstein's actual failure is not creation but abandonment: refusing to accept the obligations that come with bringing novel power into the world. The running question of the course: what does a responsible actor owe to the world it has changed? This is a question with ancient roots (Prometheus, Pandora) and contemporary urgency.

**Downstream harm attribution** — tracing responsibility through long, complex, distributed causal chains. AI systems make decisions whose consequences appear far from the point of deployment, and through intermediaries who may themselves have modified the system's outputs. Standard liability frameworks assume shorter causal chains.

**Duty of care** — the legal and ethical standard of what a responsible actor owes to those who might be harmed by its actions. Not identical to liability, but related. What counts as reasonable care when the technology is poorly understood even by its developers?

**Transparency and explainability** — accountability requires that affected parties can understand what was done and why. The black-box problem is not only a technical problem; it is an accountability problem. You cannot contest a decision you cannot understand; you cannot assign responsibility for an outcome whose mechanism is opaque.

**Voice and contestability** — the conditions under which those affected by governance decisions can challenge them. Due process, appeal mechanisms, standing to sue. Related to the question of voice in Section IV but here focused on after-the-fact correction rather than prior participation. Humanities scholars trained in questions of whose speech acts count, and under what conditions, are doing cognate analysis.

**Narrative and the assignment of responsibility** — how stories about what happened determine who is held accountable. Accident investigations, congressional hearings, and regulatory proceedings are all partly contests over narrative: what caused this, who knew what when, what was foreseeable. The power to control the authoritative account of an event is governance power. Rhetorical and historical training in how narratives are constructed and contested is directly applicable here.
