[Ben Shneiderman](https://www.hcil.umd.edu/human-centered-ai)'s take on Human-Centered AI centers on the idea that AI systems should enhance human agency and responsibility rather than replace human judgment. He argues for a framework where AI augments human capabilities while keeping humans in control of consequential decisions — what he calls "reliable, safe, and trustworthy" AI. A key distinction he draws is against "autonomy-focused" AI: instead of asking how smart we can make machines, we should ask how much better machines can make humans. He also emphasizes accountability structures, so that when AI systems act, there are humans who remain answerable for the outcomes.

Arxiv 2025 Guest, Olivia.  ["What Does 'Human-Centred AI' Mean?"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.19960)
Almost a philosophical inversion of the mainstream HCAI framing. Rather than asking "how do we design AI to be more human-centered," she argues that AI is already inherently human-centered because AI is a sociotechnical relationship in which human cognitive labour is outsourced to artifacts. The machine is never truly autonomous; there's always a human in the loop, often hidden or obfuscated (data workers, programmers, sweatshop labelers). "Centering the human" means making visible the obfuscated human labour not celebrating augmentation and recognizing that tools like LLMs are not neutral but actively harmful to the human.

Interaction Design Foundation is a website that collects design related "wisdom." "**[Human-centered AI](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/human-centered-ai)** (HCAI) refers to the **development of [artificial intelligence](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/ai "What is Artificial Intelligence (AI)?") (AI) technologies that prioritize human needs, values, and capabilities** at the core of their design and operation. This approach ensures teams create AI systems that enhance [human abilities](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/user-abilities-and-skills "What are User Abilities And Skills?") and well-being rather than replacing or diminishing human roles. It addresses AI's ethical, social, and cultural implications and ensures these systems are [accessible](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/accessibility "What is Accessibility?"), [usable](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/usability "What is Usability?"), and beneficial to all segments of society. In Human-Centered AI, designers and developers engage in interdisciplinary collaboration and often involve psychologists, ethicists and domain experts to create transparent, explainable and accountable AI. The Human-Centered AI approach aligns with the broader movement towards ethical AI and emphasizes the importance of AI systems that respect human rights, fairness, and diversity."

James Landay 2024 ["'AI For Good' Isn’t Good Enough: A Call for Human-Centered AI"](https://youtu.be/1FhgHHrhC5Q?si=pDZtwKwZupd3Uu-B)
For Landay, Human-Centered AI means expanding the scope of who you design for beyond the direct user to three nested levels: user (the individual interacting with the system), community (others affected by it — neighbors, bystanders, marginalized groups), and society (systemic, large-scale impacts if the system becomes ubiquitous). The core claim is that user-centered design alone — which HCI has been doing for 50 years — is insufficient for AI systems because their impacts radiate outward in ways that standard UX methods don't capture. His other key commitment is augmentation over replacement: AI should make people better at what they do, not simply automate them out of the picture.

The [Carnegie Mellon Human Computer Interaction Institute](https://hcii.cmu.edu/research-areas/human-centered-ai) defines it thus: "Artificial Intelligence (AI) brings amazing capabilities and challenging risks. Human-centered AI (HAI) works to maximize AI’s benefits and minimize its harms by attending to AI capabilities and their impact on users, service providers, impacted stakeholders, and society at large. This area of research: 
- Improves AI innovation processes to reduce project failure and bad outcomes
- Creates new methods for people to interact with computing systems
- Advances AI Literacy
- Informs policy and the regulation of AI systems
- Improves how innovators can team with AI to co-innovate"

In this course, we will explore the foundations of Human-Centered AI, a subdomain of artificial intelligence research that asks the core question underlying our study: How do we design AI technology in ways to augment and enhance human capability, rather than replace it outright? We will begin by examining the history of research and technology around artificial intelligence, situating our current understanding of AI in a broader historical lens that will supply some of the motivations around why it matters to design AI with a human-centric orientation. From there, we will consider some of the key concepts, tenets, and challenges of Human-Centered AI.
Four modules
- **Defining Key AI Concepts.** This module will build the foundations of our understanding of AI history, language, and current applications. We will also define many key terms that will recur throughout the lessons to help ground our discussions of AI concepts and tools.
- **The History of AI Development.** We will continue our investigations into Human-Centered AI by surveying the history of research and development in AI. We will also discuss why human-centered approaches matter now.
- **Introduction to HCAI.** In this module we will begin to investigate what human-centered AI is and where it came from. We will examine the path from symbolic systems, to machine learning, to deep learning, in order to understand what has shaped AI development over time.
- **Inserting Humans In-The-Loop.** In this module we will explore ways to design AI with humans-in-the-loop. We will begin by considering how to shape AI technology to reflect human values, which will lead us into a lesson on value-sensitive design, a vital framework for embedding human values in design practice.
