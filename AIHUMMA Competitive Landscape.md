#AIHUMMA 
(A research summary produced by Claude under direction of Dan Ryan 2/26)

CONCEIT: Program design should be motivated by what kind of graduates you are trying to produce.  One tactic for grappling with that is to imagine a set of employers and look at what kinds of folks they are hiring (and wanting to hire but perhaps not finding).  For this proof of concept, let's look at what kind of folks are hired by five AI and society organizations.

### The Five Orgs, Briefly Profiled

**[Partnership on AI](https://partnershiponai.org/about/)** — Currently no openings, but structurally they hire program managers, policy researchers, and communications staff. Strong NGO culture, expects coalition-building and stakeholder writing skills. Most hospitable to humanities people.

**[AI Now Institute](https://ainowinstitute.org/about)** — Very small, very political. Hires policy advocates and researchers with activist-adjacent sensibilities. Recent posting was a _Data Center Policy Fellow_ — essentially a community organizer who understands policy levers. The Associate Director role called for "incisive policy strategy" and "operationalizing programs." No technical requirements mentioned.

**[GovAI](https://www.governance.ai/about-us)** — The most explicitly open about wanting non-technical people. Their **Applied Track Fellowship** explicitly lists communications, policy memos, events, research management, and fundraising as viable project types. Their Research Track says "no specific requirements" and welcomes political science, history, sociology, philosophy, and law backgrounds. This is the clearest gateway program for your curriculum to target.

**[CAIS](https://safe.ai/about)** — More technically focused at the research level, but currently hiring a **Newsletter Editor** (journalism/editing skills, AI safety familiarity), a **Program Manager** (stakeholder management, fellowship/workshop/publication coordination), and an **Operations Associate** (BA required, 1-3 years experience, any background). The Program Manager role is the most instructive for curriculum: it explicitly requires no technical credentials but asks for someone who can manage "fellowships, workshops, publications, convenings, and policy initiatives."

**[METR](https://metr.org/about)** — Most technical of the five by far. Their open roles are research engineer and research scientist level, requiring Python coding ability. The one exception is an operations role. This is the hardest entry point for humanities people and probably shouldn't be the primary target for your curriculum — though understanding evaluation methodology would be valuable background.

## Implications

Across these five orgs, four competency clusters appear repeatedly in non-technical job descriptions:

**1. AI Literacy Without Coding** Every single org assumes you can _talk_ about AI systems, read technical papers with comprehension, and engage credibly with researchers and engineers — without being one. This is a distinct skill set: understanding what LLMs are and aren't, what evaluation means, what "alignment" and "safety" refer to, how training works conceptually. It's closer to science literacy than computer science. CAIS even runs an _AI Frontiers_ program explicitly tagged "no technical background required."
**2. Policy Writing and Memo Culture** GovAI's Applied Track shows this most clearly — the deliverable is a policy memo, white paper, op-ed, or communications strategy. This is a specific genre with its own conventions, quite different from academic writing. Your curriculum needs a practicum in this.
**3. Institutional Fluency** Understanding how regulatory bodies work, what government agencies do, how multi-stakeholder coalitions like PAI operate, what the relationship between civil society and industry looks like — this is the "institutional design and governance theory" you already flagged, and every org here rewards it. Not just theory but applied understanding of actual bodies: NIST, the EU AI Act, the UK AISI, the US OSTP.
**4. Research Management and Program Operations** This is the sleeper skill in your four. CAIS's Program Manager role and GovAI's Applied Track both show a real demand for people who can _run things_ — fellowships, workshops, convenings, publications. This is essentially project management with an academic/nonprofit flavor. It's a humanities-compatible competency that doesn't get discussed much in AI curriculum design but is a genuine entry point to these orgs.
## Does this suggest a program structure?
A master's certificate or program aimed at this market would probably want three layers:
**Core (everyone takes this):** AI literacy for non-engineers — what systems are, how they work conceptually, what safety/alignment/evaluation mean, how to read a capabilities report. Plus a policy writing practicum.

**Concentration A — Policy and Governance:** Institutional design, comparative AI regulation (US/EU/UK/China), stakeholder analysis, advocacy writing. Targets roles at AI Now, PAI, GovAI Research Track.

**Concentration B — Operations and Program Management:** Fellowship and convening design, research management, grant writing, communications strategy. Targets GovAI Applied Track, CAIS Program Manager, PAI program roles. This is the more "pedestrian" pipeline and probably serves a much larger labor market.

**Capstone:** A real deliverable — either a policy memo submitted to an actual organization, a fellowship application, or a program design document. GovAI's Applied Track is explicitly designed to be that capstone experience.
### Insight from Duplication
GovAI's fellowship structure is essentially already functioning as an on-ramp curriculum: it's three months of exactly what your program would be building toward. Your curriculum could explicitly position itself as _preparation for_ programs like GovAI's Applied Track, framing the fellowship as the thesis/capstone rather than building a standalone placement infrastructure. 
## Frame the Landscape Question
#### If you don't come to our program, where would you go instead?

The alternatives cluster into four different _types_ of substitute, each with a distinct weakness our program could exploit.
### Type 1: Degree-That's-Really-Technical (Disguised as Policy)

**[Brown's new MS in Data Science / AI Governance](https://graduateprograms.brown.edu/graduate-program/data-science-policy-governance-society-online-scm)** (online), **[Purdue's AI Management and Policy MS](https://www.purdue.edu/online/artificial-intelligence/ai-management-and-policy/)**, **[George Mason's AI Masters with policy track](https://getinfo-online.engineering.gwu.edu/deng-artificial-intelligence-and-machine-learning)** — these are all technically-anchored programs that added a policy flavor. Brown explicitly says students will graduate with "technical knowledge and skills." Purdue says "no coding background required" but the parent degree is an MS in Artificial Intelligence. The market signal they're sending is _you'll be credible to engineers_, which is different from _you'll be credible to policymakers_.

**The gap they leave:** Someone coming from history, political theory, or sociology doesn't see themselves in these programs. The cultural fit is wrong. The admissions pipeline is wrong — they recruit from STEM undergrads who want to add policy literacy, not from humanities students who want to add AI literacy.

See also, perhaps, [Rand AI Technology Policy](https://www.rand.edu/programs/master-technology-policy.html)
### Type 2: The Elite Research Fellowship (Not a Degree)

**GovAI's Seasonal Fellowships**, **Berkman Klein fellowships**, **CAIS's Philosophy Fellowship**, **Arcadia Impact's AI Governance Taskforce** — these are the actual on-ramps the field has built. They work, they produce real alumni, and they're where the field's talent pipeline currently runs.

**The gap they leave:** They're not degrees. They don't confer portable credentials. They're highly selective and time-limited (3 months, 1 year). They assume you're already _almost_ there — you apply with a research proposal, you're expected to hit the ground running. They're _finishers_, not _starters_. Your program would be the thing that makes someone competitive for these fellowships. That's the positioning: **GovAI Applied Track as capstone**, not as competitor.
### Type 3: The Executive Education Short Course (No Community, No Credential)

**LSE's AI Law, Policy and Governance certificate**, **Harvard Extension's ethics and governance courses**, **Chicago Harris's single-course offering** — these are real but they're courses, not programs. They serve working professionals who want a weekend or a semester of structured reading and discussion. They don't build a cohort, a network, or a portfolio.

**The gap they leave:** No community, no placement infrastructure, no demonstrated commitment to the field. You take the course and you're still an outsider who took a course.
### Type 4: The National Security Funnel (CSET and Cousins)

**CSET at Georgetown** is the closest thing to a real credentialing infrastructure for this field — it takes MA/PhD people and turns them into policy-ready researchers. But it's explicitly national security-oriented, heavily focused on US-China competition and defense implications of AI, and it effectively requires a graduate degree _plus_ 3+ years of experience to be a Research Fellow. It's a post-credential fellowship, not a degree program.

**The gap it leaves:** CSET skews toward people already inside the Beltway policy world who are adding AI expertise, not humanities people trying to enter the AI governance world from outside.
### Type 5: The Professional Certification (IAPP's AIGP)

The **AI Governance Professional (AIGP)** certification from IAPP launched in 2024. It's 100 multiple-choice questions focused on EU AI Act compliance, NIST frameworks, risk management. It's aimed at privacy lawyers, compliance officers, and corporate governance professionals who need a credential fast.

**The gap it leaves:** It's a compliance credential, not a governance/research/advocacy credential. The AIGP will get you a job in a corporate legal department auditing AI vendors. It won't get you to AI Now or GovAI. And it has no intellectual community, no cohort, no writing portfolio.
## Regional Landscape: Maryland

*Research conducted April 2026*

No Maryland institution occupies the same white space as AIHUMMA. There is no Maryland program that (a) takes humanities and social science graduates as its primary population, (b) builds intellectual community and portfolio around AI's meaning and governance, and (c) targets the civil society/NGO/research sector rather than technical roles or federal/diplomatic careers.

**Programs with partial overlap:**

**Johns Hopkins SAIS — Technology and Innovation Concentration** (within the MA in International Relations) is the closest structural neighbor in Maryland. The MAIR's Technology and Innovation concentration addresses AI governance, cyber, and synthetic biology through a policy-and-society lens, and an Emerging Technologies Initiative (launched 2023) brings together JHU and D.C.-area experts for AI governance programming. But the MAIR is a broad international relations degree training future diplomats and IR generalists — not specialist humanists. AI governance is one theme within a wider international affairs education, and the student population, career arc, and price point differ substantially.

**UMD School of Public Policy — MPP with Science & Technology Policy Track** offers a generalist 48-credit policy degree with S&T policy as one of several concentrations. UMD has also been building AI-adjacent programming including an "AI in Government" curriculum initiative and a STEP (Science, Technology, Ethics and Policy) interdisciplinary minor co-sponsored with the College of Information Studies and the Clark School of Engineering. But the MPP trains public administrators broadly — no dedicated humanities framing, no specialist AI governance depth, and no particular orientation toward civil society or NGO careers. The STEP minor is undergraduate/cross-listed and currently oversubscribed.

**UMBC — MS in Human-Centered Computing** is the most intellectually adjacent program at a non-flagship Maryland institution. HCC addresses technology design through a social and ethical lens, drawing on HCI, usability, and accessibility frameworks. But it's housed in Information Systems, admission assumes technical fluency, and career outputs are UX directors and HCI researchers — not governance or policy professionals.

**Programs that sound similar but aren't:** UMD's MSAI requires calculus, linear algebra, and programming fluency — it's an engineering-adjacent degree with some humanities-inflected offerings, precisely the "technically-anchored degree disguised as a policy program" that AIHUMMA's positioning contrasts against. Capitol Technology University's MPhil in AI is a research degree for people with existing technical backgrounds. The University of Baltimore's MS in Interaction Design has no governance or policy orientation.

Towson, Loyola Maryland, Hood College, Morgan State, and MICA have no relevant graduate programming in this space.

**Implication:** SAIS's Technology and Innovation track is the only Maryland program a prospective student might compare to AIHUMMA, but it serves a genuinely different student type. The Maryland regional landscape confirms the white-space argument made from the national scan.

## The White Space

When you map these alternatives, a genuine gap becomes visible:

> **There is no master's-level program that explicitly serves humanities/social science graduates, builds a real intellectual community and portfolio, and targets placement into the civil society / NGO / research side of AI governance** — as opposed to corporate compliance, national security, or technical research.

The closest thing is stitching together: a relevant MA (STS, public policy, political theory), followed by self-teaching on AI, followed by applying to GovAI or Berkman Klein and hoping. That's a multi-year, high-uncertainty, self-assembled pathway. Your program would replace that patchwork with a coherent credential.
## What This Means for Positioning

Our program's distinctive claims could be:

**You design for a different person.** Not the CS grad who wants to add ethics. Not the Beltway analyst who needs AI literacy. The humanities MA who has genuine theoretical depth and wants to redirect it toward one of the most consequential governance challenges of the era.

**You produce a portfolio, not just a credential.** Every competitor either produces a degree with no fieldwork, or fieldwork with no degree. Your capstone should be a real artifact — a policy memo submitted to an actual body, a fellowship application, a program design.

**You explicitly target the civil society pipeline.** GovAI Applied Track, CAIS Program Manager, AI Now Policy Fellow — these are your placement targets. Name them in your materials. The field doesn't have a feeder program for these roles the way law schools feed into law firms. You'd be building that.

**You're upstream of the fellowships, not competing with them.** Students graduate and immediately apply to GovAI's Applied Track or Berkman Klein. The fellowship _is_ the placement. You're not building a separate placement office — you're building the credential that makes those applications competitive.
