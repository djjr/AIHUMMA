The Case
A shortage that is specific, not generic

What the field needs

The organizations doing the most consequential work in AI governance — GovAI, CSET, AI Now, CAIS, PAI, and a growing set of government offices and think tanks — are understaffed. But the shortage is not of technical talent.

It is a shortage of people who bring the training of the social sciences and humanities to AI problems: comparative institutional analysis, interpretive and ethnographic methods, rhetorical and ethical frameworks. These are not soft supplements to technical understanding.

They are the tools the governance problems actually require.

What you bring

If you are trained in political science, history, sociology, philosophy, or the humanities, you have already developed the analytical capacities the field most needs.

Understanding how technologies transform institutions. How narratives of inevitability get constructed and contested. How power operates through systems that appear neutral. How policy arguments are made, evaluated, and contested.

AIHUMMA gives you the technical fluency and professional formation to bring that training to bear where it matters.

Program Design
Three design principles
1
Humanities and social sciences as the analytical core — not decoration

The program is built around what you already know how to do. Technical literacy is added to it, not the other way around. The direction of the degree is from your strengths outward.

2
Technical literacy built from the governance artifacts you will actually use

Model cards, capability evaluations, responsible scaling policies — not ML textbooks. The same underlying concepts, reoriented for a different purpose and a different professional destination.

3
A program arc that cannot be assembled from electives

The sequencing and scaffolding that produce deep competency are not properties of individual courses. The skill of reading a technical AI document critically (F1) must become policy writing (F4) before it can reach its highest application (A3). That arc has to be built.

Stage 1

F1
How AI Systems Work

Technical concepts through governance artifacts, not textbooks

Signature · TL-02  —  Reading technical AI docs as arguments

Teaches ML, LLMs, and AI safety concepts through model cards, capability evaluations, and Responsible Scaling Policies rather than ML textbooks. Students learn to read technical AI documentation as argument, not neutral fact — the foundational skill that every other course in the program builds on. The course nobody else teaches.

Institutions, Governance & the Regulation of Risky Technologies

Nuclear, pharma, aviation, finance as analytical lenses for AI

Signature · GP-03  —  Historical governance analogies

A full semester on the comparative governance question that other courses give two weeks. Uses nuclear, pharmaceutical, aviation, and financial regulation as analytical lenses. Asks not just what each regime says but why different political economies produced different regulatory forms — the comparative institutional logic that GP-01 survey courses never reach.

Ethics, Values & AI: From Philosophy to Policy

The ethics course that actually gets to Part 4

Signature · EV-05  —  Procedural vs. structural critique

Builds through frameworks → standards → institutional ethics → then the move most ethics courses avoid: distinguishing procedural ethics from structural critique. Makes the Timnit Gebru / AI Now "ethics washing" argument into a learnable analytical skill. EV-05 appears in only 1 course across the full 65-course institutional inventory.

A1
Comparative AI Regulation

EU, US, UK, China, Global South — comparative political economy, not doctrinal comparison

Signature · GP-06  —  International AI competition & cooperation

Asks why the EU produced binding regulation while the US produced voluntary frameworks — which requires comparative political economy, not just doctrinal comparison. Deep institutional analysis of the EU AI Act, US Executive Order, UK soft-law approach, China's algorithmic regulation regime, and Global South dynamics. Research Methods for AI Governance

The craft of research GovAI and CSET actually publish

Signature · RM-01  —  Qualitative research for AI governance

Elite interviewing, document analysis, process tracing, and humanistic close reading applied to AI governance questions. A3
Seminar: Frontier AI Risk & Governance

Reads both the longtermists and the critics — and requires students to engage both seriously

Signature · GP-05  —  Technical AI safety from a governance standpoint

The capstone of Track A's intellectual arc. Dual-literature design: students read Ord, Christiano, and METR evaluation reports alongside Gebru, Crawford, and AI Now critiques. Primary materials are the exact governance artifacts F1 taught students to read critically — now applied at full difficulty. Requires students to form defensible views about where these literatures converge and conflict.

B1
Program Design & Convening

Fellowship design, workshop architecture, and field-building as intellectual craft

Signature · RM-04  —  Evidence synthesis for program design

Designing programs, fellowships, and convenings as a learnable craft — drawing on organizational sociology, adult learning theory, and the institutional history of convening bodies like Asilomar and the Santa Fe Institute. The core argument: good program design requires the same evidence synthesis and defensible-argument construction skills as good policy research.

P1
Field Practicum

8 weeks embedded in a partner organization on a real project

Core competencies · CW-01 + RM-04

8-week engagement with a partner organization — GovAI, CAIS, AI Now, a city-level AI governance body, or a federal AI office. Every student produces policy-quality writing and synthesizes evidence into a real deliverable for a real organizational client. Track-dependent: A-track placements emphasize research outputs; B-track placements emphasize communications and program work.

P2
Capstone Seminar

Faculty-mentored project development with weekly practitioner guests

Core competencies · RM-04 + CW-01

Every session includes a practitioner from a target organization who responds directly to student work in progress — creating real intellectual exchanges and hiring relationships, not networking events. Students develop capstone projects with peer critique and faculty mentorship. The practitioner engagement model is the course's distinctive contribution: it makes program completion an entry point into the field.
